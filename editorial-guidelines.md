# Editorial guidelines

Short version: **be practical, be honest, cite your sources, and respect patient privacy.**

## For clinical articles

- **Write for a peer, not a layperson.** Assume the reader is a dentist or endodontist. Don't explain what "apical foramen" means unless the whole article is glossary-style.
- **Prefer evidence over opinion.** If you state a technique works, link to a study. If it's your personal preference, say so.
- **Cite properly.** Use footnotes: `[^tag]` in the body, `[^tag]: Full citation.` at the bottom of the article. The footnote plugin renders them nicely.
- **Short sections, clear headings.** Use `##` for main sections, `###` for subsections. Don't go deeper than `####`.
- **Link liberally.** If you mention another topic that has its own article on EndoWiki, link to it.
- **No patient photos or radiographs with identifying information.** Crop, redact, or skip.

### Article structure (a good default)

1. **Lead paragraph** — what the topic is, why it matters, in 2–3 sentences
2. **Indications / background**
3. **Technique / content** — broken into clear subsections
4. **Outcomes / evidence**
5. **See also** — links to related EndoWiki articles
6. **References** — footnotes with real citations

## For reviews

**Honesty is the whole point.** Readers can get marketing copy from any manufacturer website — EndoWiki is the place for what it's actually like to use the thing.

- **Disclose conflicts.** If the manufacturer gave you the course for free, if you're a paid KOL, if you teach for the company — say so at the top of the review.
- **Rate fairly.** 5 stars means "essentially no complaints, would buy/attend again." 1 star means "actively regret this." Be willing to use the middle of the scale.
- **Cover both pros and cons.** A 5-star review with zero cons is suspicious. Even great products have drawbacks.
- **Include price.** Even approximate. Price-to-value is half the decision for buyers.
- **Would recommend?** Yes / no / mixed. This is the headline. Back it up in the verdict sentence.

### What to include in a course review

- Provider (institution or company)
- Instructor(s)
- Format (in-person, online live, online self-paced, hybrid)
- Duration (hours, days, weeks)
- Price (approximate, in USD or EUR, note if CE/CPD credits are included)
- Location (if in-person)
- Pros / cons (bullet lists)
- Would recommend?
- Who the course is a good fit for (beginner? advanced? specific case types?)

### What to include in an equipment review

- Brand and model
- Category (apex locator, motor, microscope, file system, etc.)
- Price (approximate, note region if pricing varies)
- How long you've used it
- What you were using before (comparison is useful)
- Pros / cons (bullet lists)
- Reliability — any failures, warranty experiences
- Would recommend? For what use case?

## Tone

- **Neutral point of view.** For articles, present evidence, note disagreements, don't pick sides unless the evidence clearly does.
- **First-person is fine in reviews, not in articles.** Reviews are personal experience. Articles are collective knowledge.
- **No promotional language.** No "revolutionary", "game-changing", "must-have" unless you're quoting someone else.
- **Don't bash.** Criticize the thing, not the company — and criticize with specifics, not vibes.
