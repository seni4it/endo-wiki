*Disclosure: Purchased through a standard dealer contract. No relationship with Zeiss beyond being a paying customer.*

## What I was using before

A 2014 Global G6 that I still own for the second operatory. The Extaro is a step up in optical quality and a significant step up in illumination versatility. It is **not** a step up in magnification range or ergonomics beyond what a well-specced Global already provides.

## In use

Optics: the reason you buy Zeiss. Apochromatic correction means color under high magnification is true to life, which matters for distinguishing sclerotic dentine from caries, old composite from natural tooth, and subtle tissue tone changes. On the Global I occasionally second-guessed a color boundary; on the Extaro I don't.

Illumination modes take time. The white light is superb. The amber/orange filter mode reduces composite curing during a long restorative case — you can work under it without risk of premature set. The fluorescence mode highlights caries (with plaque stained by the manufacturer's indicator) and biofilm in the pulp chamber in a way that genuinely changed how I retreat certain cases. This is the feature that non-Zeiss users often dismiss as marketing; it is not. It is legitimately useful on specific case types.

Documentation camera: built-in 4K, writes to a local SD card, no PC required. A clip from a case of mine took 15 seconds from "interesting view" to "on the SD card." That's meaningful when you're trying to build a case library across years.

## Reliability

18 months, zero clinical failures. The main complaint is service responsiveness — a routine calibration visit took six weeks to schedule and two hours to complete. Nothing urgent, but Zeiss is not as responsive as some competitors for non-emergency service in my region.

## Value

Here is the honest part. The price premium over a good competitor (Global, Leica M320, CJ Optik) is significant. The optics and illumination modes are real advantages but they are incremental, not transformational. If you're shopping on a budget:

- **Starting out:** do not buy a new Zeiss. A used Global G6 or a new Leica M320 is the right first microscope.
- **Upgrading from a basic scope and you can justify the capex:** the Extaro is excellent.
- **Already have a good microscope that works:** the case to upgrade is weak unless you specifically want the fluorescence mode.

## Verdict

**Recommended with reservations.** The Extaro is the best microscope I have used. It is also an expensive way to get incremental gains over a solid competitor. Buy it when you've outgrown a basic unit and can justify the premium. Do not buy it as your first microscope.
