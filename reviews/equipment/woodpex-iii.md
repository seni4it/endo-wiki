## What I was using before

A first-generation Root ZX (bench loaner for years, then borrowed from colleagues). The Woodpex III is a direct personal upgrade; I wanted my own device on my own cart.

## In use

Accuracy is the headline. I cross-checked dozens of readings against a Root ZX II and against post-op radiographs. In uncomplicated cases — stable readings on dried canals with a small pre-flared file — the Woodpex III tracks the Root ZX within the same half-millimeter gradation. If you pick a device for routine endodontic cases and price is a real consideration, there is genuinely no accuracy reason to pay three times as much.

The color display is bright enough to read from the assistant's chair. The progression bar + the numerical "DISTANCE TO APEX" readout give two ways to read the same thing, which helps when you're angled away from the unit.

## Reliability

Six months of daily use, zero issues. Battery still holds its charge over a long weekend. I've dropped the handpiece unit from about chair height onto a tile floor (not recommended) and it still works. Lip clips and file clips show no wear; they're standard replaceable.

## Value

At ~$140, this sits in "buy a spare" territory for most practices. A backup apex locator for the price of a single high-end obturation system is a sensible defensive purchase.

Downsides vs. higher-end devices:

- Slightly worse behavior in very wet fields (hypochlorite pooling in the chamber will confuse it; this is also true of higher-end devices to a lesser degree)
- Narrower range of useful function in immature / wide-open apices

## Verdict

**Strongly recommended for general dentists and for specialists who want a backup unit.** If I were starting a practice today, this would be my primary apex locator, and I would not feel any technical deficit compared to colleagues running Root ZX II units. If I were doing mostly pediatric or resorption cases with wide-open apices, I might still prefer a higher-end device for the edge cases.
