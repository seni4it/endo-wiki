# Rotary vs reciprocating files: what the evidence shows

**Short version:** both systems produce clinically comparable outcomes when used correctly. The "best" system is the one you are fluent with — operator familiarity beats small differences in file design.

## What each does

### Rotary

A nickel–titanium (NiTi) file rotates continuously (360°) in the canal. Shaping is the result of sustained cutting action along the file's flutes.

Examples: ProTaper Gold, Vortex Blue, BioRaCe, HyFlex EDM, TruNatomy.

### Reciprocating

A NiTi file moves in an asymmetric back-and-forth motion — typically a larger cutting rotation counterclockwise (~150°) followed by a smaller release rotation clockwise (~30°). The file cuts on the engagement stroke and disengages on the release stroke, reducing stress concentration.

Examples: WaveOne Gold, Reciproc Blue, Procodile.

## Claimed advantages

| Rotary | Reciprocating |
| --- | --- |
| Cleaner shaping of curved canals | Fewer file separations in narrow/curved canals |
| Wider range of tapers and tip sizes per system | Typically single-file protocol → faster |
| More natural for operators trained in hand instrumentation | Less operator fatigue |
| Better for retreatment (more file choices) | Strong primary-shaping tools |

## What the evidence actually shows

### Apical transportation and centering

Modern heat-treated rotary and reciprocating files perform **similarly** for canal centering in moderate curvatures. Extreme curvatures (>35°) favor reciprocating systems in some in-vitro studies[^burklein-transportation].

### File separation

Reciprocating motion reduces cyclic fatigue compared to continuous rotation at similar stress levels. However, **most separations are caused by operator error** (apical binding, excessive apical pressure, neglecting patency), not the motion itself. Separation rates in experienced hands are <1% for both.

### Debris extrusion

Reciprocating systems push slightly more debris apically in laboratory studies, but the magnitude is small and of unclear clinical relevance[^burklein-debris].

### Treatment time

Single-file reciprocating protocols are faster per canal by ~30–60 seconds on average. Over a molar case, the difference is marginal compared to irrigation and obturation time.

### Clinical outcomes

Randomized controlled trials comparing rotary and reciprocating systems show **no statistically significant difference in healing rates or symptom resolution** at 1 and 2 years[^nair-rct]. Outcomes are dominated by irrigation quality and coronal restoration, not file choice.

## Practical guidance

- **If you're learning:** pick one system from either category and learn it thoroughly. Don't switch systems every six months — you'll never reach proficiency.
- **If you're experienced with one:** switching to the other buys very little outcome benefit. Evaluate on ergonomics, workflow, and cost.
- **If you do a lot of retreatment:** a rotary system with varied tapers is often more useful than a single-file reciprocating protocol.
- **Never reuse a file** that has been through a difficult case, regardless of system. Heat-treated alloys gain ductility at the cost of visible shape memory loss — you cannot tell by looking that a file is about to fail.

## Budget reality

Most endodontic failures are not caused by file choice. In descending order of impact:

1. Coronal restoration quality
2. Isolation (rubber dam, maintained throughout)
3. Irrigation volume, contact time, and agitation
4. Adequate shaping (any reasonable system)
5. Obturation technique
6. Choice of file system

If you're optimizing, start at the top of the list.
