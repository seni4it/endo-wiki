# Root canal treatment — an overview

**Root canal treatment (RCT)**, also known as endodontic therapy, is the clinical procedure that removes infected or inflamed dental pulp, disinfects the root canal system, and seals it to prevent reinfection. Done well, it preserves a tooth that would otherwise need extraction.

> **Scope of this article:** a practical overview for clinicians. For patient-facing information, write a separate article under *Patient information*. For specific techniques, link out to the dedicated articles.

## Indications

- Irreversible pulpitis (confirmed clinically, not just by symptoms)
- Pulp necrosis, with or without periapical pathology
- Teeth requiring post-and-core build-up where the pulp is compromised
- Elective devitalization for restorative reasons (rare, debated)

Contraindications are largely anatomical or prosthetic: unrestorable tooth structure, severe periodontal compromise, vertical root fracture, or teeth in a failing strategic position.

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis of pulpal and periapical disease follows the [AAE diagnostic terminology](https://www.aae.org/specialty/clinical-resources/glossary-endodontic-terms/). Key categories:

| Pulpal diagnosis | Periapical diagnosis |
| --- | --- |
| Normal pulp | Normal apical tissues |
| Reversible pulpitis | Symptomatic apical periodontitis |
| Symptomatic irreversible pulpitis | Asymptomatic apical periodontitis |
| Asymptomatic irreversible pulpitis | Chronic apical abscess |
| Pulp necrosis | Acute apical abscess |
| Previously treated / Previously initiated | Condensing osteitis |

Always record **both** a pulpal and a periapical diagnosis.

## Isolation

Rubber dam is the standard of care. The European Society of Endodontology and the AAE both consider it non-negotiable for endodontic treatment[^ese-quality]. Use a split-dam technique for difficult cases.

## Access and working length

- Conservative access preserves pericervical dentine and improves long-term survival.
- Use [apex locators](articles/apex-locators.md) to establish working length; confirm radiographically only when the reading is unstable or anatomy is unusual.

## Shaping

Shaping removes infected dentine and creates space for irrigation. The choice between [rotary vs reciprocating](articles/rotary-vs-reciprocating.md) systems is operator-dependent — both achieve adequate shapes when used correctly.

Key principles regardless of system:

- Maintain apical patency with small hand files between rotary steps
- Avoid apical transportation — the apical third is least tolerant of deviation
- Irrigate between every instrument change

## Irrigation

See the dedicated article on [irrigation protocols](articles/irrigation-protocols.md). Core points:

- Sodium hypochlorite (1–5.25%) for tissue dissolution and antimicrobial activity
- EDTA (17%) as final rinse to remove the smear layer
- Agitate (sonic, ultrasonic, or multisonic) — static irrigant is insufficient

## Obturation

Goal: entomb the cleaned canal space and create a coronal-apical seal. Techniques:

- **Cold lateral compaction** — predictable, less operator-variable, but generates less homogeneous fills
- **Warm vertical compaction** (Schilder) — better adaptation to canal irregularities
- **Carrier-based** — fast, but risk of coronal displacement and quality depends on system

## Restoration

**The coronal seal matters as much as the root filling.** Place a definitive restoration — direct or indirect — promptly. Leaving a temporary restoration for more than a few weeks significantly increases the risk of reinfection and failure[^ray-trope].

## Outcomes

Modern literature puts single-visit nonsurgical endodontic treatment success at **~90% for vital teeth** and **~80% for teeth with pre-existing apical periodontitis**, with follow-up of 2–4 years[^ng-outcomes]. Success depends heavily on:

- Adequate coronal restoration (arguably the single strongest predictor)
- Absence of pre-operative periapical lesion
- Rubber dam isolation
- Adequate shaping, disinfection, and obturation
